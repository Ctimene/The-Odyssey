Summary of The Odyssey

This page presents a concise summary of the entire epic, along with individual summaries for each book. Both formats outline the principal events and characters, offering an overview of Odysseus’s journey—one in a more condensed form than the other.

Summary of Entire Epic:

Note: The following summary isn't in chronological order as presented in The Odyssey, but rather in the order of Odysseus's journey.
1. After fighting for ten years in the Trojan War, Odysseus, King of Ithaca, goes on a ten-year journey home, plagued by battles and curses from Gods and monsters.
2. At the twenty-year point of Odysseus’s journey, his son, Telemachus, goes on his own journey to figure out his father’s whereabouts with the aid of Athena.
3. Once Odysseus finally returns home, after losing all six hundred of his men to monsters, Gods, and divine punishment, he finds his home overrun with suitors attempting to take his wife’s hand in marriage.

Summary of Each Book:

Book 1: Athena persuades her father, Zeus, to allow Odysseus to come home. Athena, in disguise, meets with and convinces Telemachus to go on a journey to Pylos and Sparta to find information about his father.

Book 2: Telemachus holds an assembly to complain of the conduct of the suitors, with the suitors complaining in turn of his mother’s actions to delay picking a new husband. Athena aids Telemachus in setting sail for Pylos.

Book 3: Reaching Pylos, Telemachus hears stories from King Nestor about their departure from Troy, the murder of Agamemnon, and Menelaus's homecoming. Nestor sends his son, Peisistratus, to accompany Telemachus to Sparta.

Book 4: Telemachus and Peisistratus arrive in Sparta to meet Menelaus and Helen, who invite them in with stories of Troy, and Odysseus’s captivity on Ogygia, the island of the nymph, Calypso. The suitors hear of Telemachus’s departure, planning an ambush attack for when he returns. Penelope learn of Telemachus’s journey, grieving before being comforted by a dream from Athena.

Book 5: Athena urges Zeus to send the messenger god, Hermes, to Calypso to allow Odysseus to leave. Once building a raft, Odysseus sets forth for Scheria, the land of the Phaeacians. Still angered by Odysseus, Poseidon destroys his raft, though Odysseus manages to make it to shore.

Book 6: Nausicaa, the daughter of the Phaeacian king, is inspired by Athena to go to the river to wash clothes. Odysseus appears, startling Nausicaa and her attendants, and begs for help. She gives him clothes and advises him on how to reach the palace of her father, Alcinous.

Book 7: After arriving at the palace of Alcinous, Odysseus pleads with the queen, Arete, for help. Alcinous orders a feast for Odysseus, promising to arrange Odysseus’s return. Without revealing his identity, Odysseus speaks of his captivity on Calypso’s island. Alcinous promises his daughter’s hand in marriage or Odysseus’s safe return home, if he truly desires it.

Book 8: At the Phaeacian feast, the singer, Demodocus, sings of the Trojan horse after their athletic contest where Odysseus proves himself after being challenged. When Odysseus is moved to tears, he is asked to reveal his name and tell why he wept over the stories of Troy.

Book 9: Odysseus identifies himself and begins speaking of his wandering, beginning with his departure from Troy and the raid of the Cicones. Then of their visit to the island of the Lotus-Eaters, whose food made his men forget their home. He describes the land of the Cyclops. They were held captive by Polyphemus and escaped using a trick. They described how they provoked Poseidon's deep anger with Odysseus.

Book 10: Odysseus describes how they reached the island of the wind god, Aeolus, who gave them a wind bag to help them get home. When they were close to home, Odysseus’s men opened the wind bag, sending them right back to Aeolos’ island. After Aeolos refused to help them, they reached the land of the Laestrygonians, who destroyed the entire fleet except for Odysseus’s ship. Reaching the island of Circe, Odysseus’s men were turned into pigs by the enchantress, with Odysseus managing to beat Circe with a charm given by Hermes. Odysseus and his men are entertained by Circe for a year before leaving for the Underworld to find the prophet Teiresias on Circe’s instruction.

Book 11: In the Underworld, Odysseus meets with Teiresias, hearing warnings of touching Helios’s cattle, his homecoming and the one last journey he would have to make. After being interrupted with praise from Queen Arete after speaking of his conversation with Teiresias about his mother, Anticlea, and other noblewomen, King Alcinous urges Odysseus to continue speaking of his conversations with Greek heroes, such as Agamemnon and Achilles.

Book 12: Odysseus continues to speak of his return to Circe’s island and her words of cautious. How he heard the Sirens’ songs when passing by them, tied to the mast. Their fight between Charybdis, the whirlpool, and Scylla, who killed six of Odysseus’s men. At the insistence of Eurylochus, the second in command, they landed on Thrinacia, the island of Helios, and ended up stranded there for a month due to storms. After slaughtering Helios’s cattle against Odysseus warning, while he was sleeping, they attempt to escape, only to be caught by Zeus who drowns everyone but Odysseus, who ends up on Calypso's island.

Book 13: The Phaeacians brought Odysseus to Ithaca and left him sleeping on the shore. On their return, Poseidon turned their ship to stone. Athena comes to Odysseus in disguise, telling him of the events unfolding at home. Odysseus conceals his identity, lying about killing he killing Idomeneus' son on Crete and fleeing the island after the Trojan War. Once Athena reveals her identity, she advises Odysseus on how to overcome the suitors and disguises him as an old beggar.

Book 14: Odysseus goes to the house of Eumaeus, his loyal swineherd. Odysseus tells him a fake story about his adventures has a Cretan warrior who led troops in Troy, and then journeying to Egypt, Phoenicia and other places. Later telling another false story about his adventures at Troy to win a cloak from him.

Book 15: Urged by Athena, Telemachus leaves Sparta, and sailing home from Pylos, taking Theoclymenus, a fugitive prophet, with him. At Ithaca, Eumaeus urges the disguised Odysseus to wait for Telemachus before going to beg from the suitors at the banquet. Eumaeus answers questions about his home, his father and tells Odysseus about being kidnapped by a Phoenician servant, enslaved and purchased by Laertes. All while, Telemachus reaches Ithaca and manages to evade the suitors’ ambush.

Book 16: Telemachus visits Eumaeus and sends him to inform Penelope of his arrival. Odysseus reveals his identity to Telemachus, and they plan their revenge on the suitors. Penelope and the suitors are informed of Telemachus’s return, and the suitors decide whether they should kill him, only to be rebuked by Penelope for their plot.

Book 17: Telemachus returns to his home, telling Penelope of his journey, with the seer, Theoclymenus, prophesying Odysseus’ imminent return. Odysseus, disguised as a beggar, goes to his home with Eumaeus. Meeting Argos, Odysseus’ old dog, who recognizes Odysseus before he dies of old age. Odysseus begs from the suitors, telling them false stories of his adventures. Antinous, the leader of the suitors, abuses Odysseus, hurling a stool at him. Eumaeus tells Penelope of the “stranger” and his stories.

Book 18: The beggar, Iros, arrives and challenges Odysseus, finding himself beaten by him. Odysseus warns Amphinomus, one of the suitors, of their reckless behavior. Penelope comes down, complaining of the suitors’ behaviour. The feast breaks up in chaos, after Odysseus angers Eurymachus, one of the suitors.

Book 19: Odysseus and Telemachus remove the weapons and armour from the hall, and Penelope questions Odysseus. He tells her a false story and they speak. The nurse, Eurycleia, washes Odysseus’ feet and recognizes him from his scar. Penelope proposes to set up a contest for the suitors with Odysseus’ bow, with her marrying the winner.

Book 20: The next day, the suitors gather in Odysseus home, with the suitors beginning their plot to kill Telemachus, with the prophet, Theoclymenus, forseeing the end for the suitors.

Book 21: Penelope announces the contest to the suitors, with them failing to string the bow. Meanwhile, Odysseus quietly reveals himself to his two loyal servants, Eumaeus and Philoetius, and despite the suitors’ protest, Odysseus it given the bow, which he strings and shoots through the row of axes.

Book 22: Odysseus shoot Antinous, realing himself. The battle begins, and, with Athena aid, the suitors are killed. While the unfaithful maids and Melanthios are brutally punished.

Book 23: Eurycleia reveals to Penelope that Odysseus returned and defeated the suitors, with Penelope skeptical and choosing to test Odysseus with the story of their bed. She recognizes him from his response and they are reunited, recounting their trials to one another.

Book 24: In the Underworld, Hermes leads the suitors to the land of the dead, where Agamemnon and Achilles are talking about each other’s deaths. The ghosts of the suitors arrive and describe their fate to Odysseus, with Agamemnon praising Odysseus’ wife for her faithfulness, while cursing his own wife, Clytemnestra. Meanwhile, Odysseus goes to his father, Laertes, and reveals himself. The suitors’ relatives plan revenge and after one of them is killed, Athena intervenes to make peace. In the end, Odysseus sacrifices a ram, bull and boar to Poseidon as an apology for the damage he caused.
